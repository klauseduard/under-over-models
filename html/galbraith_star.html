Galbraith's Star Model

Strategy
Determines direction through goals, objectives, values, and mission. Defines the criteria for selecting organizational activities.
Examples:
  • Mission and vision statements
  • Strategic objectives and goals
  • Competitive positioning and market focus
  • Value propositions
  • Business model definition

Structure
Determines the location of formal power and authority. Involves decisions about specialization, distribution of power, departmentalization, and shape.
Examples:
  • Hierarchy levels and reporting relationships
  • Centralized vs. decentralized decision-making
  • Functional vs. divisional organization
  • Matrix or network structures
  • Span of control considerations
Organizational Review:
Processes
Information and decision processes that cut across the organizational structure. The flow of information and decisions for work activities.
Examples:
  • Planning and budgeting processes
  • Information systems and communication flows
  • Decision-making processes
  • Resource allocation mechanisms
  • Performance management systems
Organizational Review:
Rewards
Aligns individual goals with organizational objectives. Includes compensation, recognition, promotion systems, and other incentives.
Examples:
  • Compensation and salary structures
  • Bonus and incentive systems
  • Recognition and appreciation programs
  • Career advancement opportunities
  • Non-monetary benefits
Organizational Review:
People
Human resource policies that influence and define employees' mindsets and skills. Includes recruitment, development, and culture-building activities.
Examples:
  • Recruitment and selection practices
  • Training and development programs
  • Leadership development
  • Workforce planning
  • Organizational culture initiatives

How to Use This Model

Jay Galbraith's Star Model emphasizes that all five elements must be aligned for organizational effectiveness. When one element changes, all others usually need to be adjusted.

  1. Click on each star point or navigation button to learn about that element and document your current practices.
  2. Evaluate alignment between elements - misalignment often causes organizational problems.
  3. Consider how changes in one element would require adjustments in others to maintain alignment.
  4. Use this tool both for diagnosing organizational issues and for planning organizational change.
